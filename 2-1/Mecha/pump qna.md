**Negative slip:**
In most reciprocating pumps, the actual discharge ($Q_{act.}$) is less than the theoretical discharge ($Q_{th.}$); in such a case, the value of the coefficient of discharge ($C_d$) is less than unity, and the slip of the pump is 'positive'.

The expression for work done or energy supplied by the impeller of a centrifugal pump on the liquid flowing through may be derived in the same way as for turbine.

$\omega$ = Angular velocity $\left( = \frac{2\pi N}{60} \text{ rad/s} \right)$,

Torque on the impeller = Rate of change of moment of momentum
Moment of momentum at inlet = $0$ (Assuming liquid enters the impeller eye in radial direction, the whirl component $V_{w1}$ is zero)
Moment of momentum at outlet = $\frac{W}{g} (V_{w2} R_2)$

Work done per second = $\frac{W}{g} (V_{w2} u_2 - V_{w1} u_1)$

$= \frac{1}{g} (V_{w2} u_2 - V_{w1} u_1)$

### (b) Discuss the function of air vessels in a reciprocating pump.

An air vessel is a closed chamber containing compressed air in the upper part and liquid being pumped in the lower part. One air vessel is fixed on the suction pipe just near the suction valve and one is fixed on the delivery pipe near the delivery valve. 

The air vessels are used for the following purposes:
1. **To get continuous supply of liquid at a uniform rate** (whatever fluctuations take place, they occur between the air vessels and the pump).
2. **To save the power required to drive the pump** (By the use of air vessels the acceleration and friction heads are considerably reduced, thereby the work is also reduced).

### b) Define specific speed of a centrifugal pump. How is the model testing of the centrifugal pump carried out?

**Definition of Specific Speed:**
The specific speed of a centrifugal pump is defined as the speed of a geometrically similar pump which would deliver unit quantity (one cubic metre of liquid per second) against a unit head (one metre). It is denoted by $N_s$. The specific speed is a characteristic of pumps which can be used as a basis for comparing the performance of different pumps.
It is given by the formula:
$$N_s = \frac{N\sqrt{Q}}{(H_{mano})^{3/4}}$$

**Model Testing of Centrifugal Pumps:**
In order to know the performance of prototypes, the models of centrifugal pumps are tested. The performance of the prototype pump will be correctly predicted by its model test only if the following conditions of complete similarity are satisfied:

1.  **Specific speed** of the model must equal the specific speed of the prototype:
    $$(N_s)_m = (N_s)_p$$
    $$\left[ \frac{N\sqrt{Q}}{(H_{mano})^{3/4}} \right]_m = \left[ \frac{N\sqrt{Q}}{(H_{mano})^{3/4}} \right]_p$$

2.  **Head co-efficient ($C_H$)** must be constant:
    $$\left( \frac{\sqrt{H_{mano}}}{DN} \right)_m = \left( \frac{\sqrt{H_{mano}}}{DN} \right)_p \text{ or } \left( \frac{H_{mano}}{D^2 N^2} \right)_m = \left( \frac{H_{mano}}{D^2 N^2} \right)_p$$

3.  **Capacity or flow co-efficient ($C_Q$)** must be constant:
    $$\left( \frac{Q}{D^3 N} \right)_m = \left( \frac{Q}{D^3 N} \right)_p$$

4.  **Power co-efficient ($C_P$)** must be constant:
    $$\left( \frac{P}{D^5 N^3} \right)_m = \left( \frac{P}{D^5 N^3} \right)_p$$

### (a) Derive an expression of Discharge for single and double acting reciprocating pumps.

**(i) Single-acting reciprocating pump**
Consider a single-acting reciprocating pump.
Let,
$D$ = Diameter of the cylinder, m
$A$ = Cross-sectional area of the piston/cylinder $= \frac{\pi}{4} D^2 \text{ m}^2$
$L$ = Length of the stroke, m
$N$ = Speed of the crank, r.p.m.

Volume of liquid sucked in during suction stroke $= A \times L$
Since it is a single-acting pump, water is delivered only once in each revolution.
Discharge of the pump per minute $= A \times L \times N$
$\therefore$ Discharge of the pump per second, **$Q = \frac{ALN}{60}$**

**(ii) Double-acting Reciprocating Pump**
Let,
$D$ = Diameter of the piston,
$d$ = Diameter of the piston rod,
$A_{pr}$ = cross-sectional area of the piston rod $= \frac{\pi}{4} d^2$

Area on one side of the piston, $A = \frac{\pi}{4} D^2$
Area on other side of the piston where piston rod is connected to the piston,
$A' = A - A_{pr} = \frac{\pi}{4} D^2 - \frac{\pi}{4} d^2 = \frac{\pi}{4} (D^2 - d^2)$.

Volume of liquid delivered in one revolution of crank
$= A L + A' L = (A + A') L = \left[ \frac{\pi}{4} D^2 + \frac{\pi}{4} (D^2 - d^2) \right] L$

$\therefore$ Discharge of the pump per second **$= \left[ \frac{\pi}{4} D^2 + \frac{\pi}{4} (D^2 - d^2) \right] L \times \frac{N}{60}$**

If the diameter of the piston rod '$d$' is very small as compared to the diameter of the piston '$D$' then it can be neglected and hence discharge of the pump per second will become
**$Q = 2 \times \frac{ALN}{60}$**

### (b) Describe the working principle of Double acting reciprocating pump.

In a double-acting reciprocating pump, suction and delivery strokes occur simultaneously on opposite sides of the piston. 

1. When the crank rotates from the Inner Dead Centre (I.D.C.) in the clockwise direction, a vacuum is created on the left side of piston and the liquid is sucked in from the sump through the suction valve $S_1$. 
2. At the same time, the liquid on the right side of the piston is pressed and a high pressure causes the delivery valve $D_2$ to open and the liquid is passed on to the discharge tank. This operation continues till the crank reaches Outer Dead Centre (O.D.C.).
3. With further rotation of the crank (from O.D.C. back to I.D.C.), the piston moves from right to left. Now, the liquid is sucked in from the sump through the suction valve $S_2$ on the right side.
4. Simultaneously, the liquid on the left side is pressed, the delivery valve $D_1$ opens, and the liquid is delivered to the discharge tank. 
5. When the crank reaches I.D.C., the piston is in the extreme left position, completing one cycle.

Because of continuous delivery strokes (delivery happens during both forward and backward strokes of the piston), a double-acting reciprocating pump gives a more uniform discharge compared to a single-acting pump.
### What is priming? Why is it necessary?

Based on section "3.19. PRIMING OF A CENTRIFUGAL PUMP" from the textbook:

**What is priming:**
The operation of filling the suction pipe, casing of the pump and a portion of the delivery pipe completely from outside source with the liquid to be raised, before starting the pump, to remove any air, gas or vapour from these parts of the pump is called priming of a centrifugal pump.

**Why it is necessary:**
If a centrifugal pump is not primed before starting, air pockets inside the impeller may give rise to vortices and cause discontinuity of flow. Further, dry running of the pump may result in rubbing and seizing of the wearing rings and cause serious damage.

### (a) Differentiate between centrifugal pump and reciprocating pump.

Based on the provided text, the differences between a centrifugal pump and a reciprocating (displacement) pump can be established from their respective advantages, working principles, and typical applications:

1.  **Basic Principle:** In a centrifugal pump, flow takes place from the low-pressure side towards the higher pressure side resulting in decelerated flow, utilizing a combination of centrifugal, pressure, and kinetic energy. A reciprocating pump is a positive displacement pump that sucks and raises liquid by actually displacing it with a piston/plunger executing a reciprocating motion.
2.  **Discharge Characteristics:** The discharging capacity of a centrifugal pump is much greater than that of a reciprocating pump. A centrifugal pump provides a uniform output, whereas a single-acting reciprocating pump pumps liquid intermittently.
3.  **Suitability based on Head and Capacity:** Reciprocating pumps are best suited for relatively small capacities and high heads. Centrifugal pumps cover a wider range but are generally more advantageous for larger discharges.
4.  **Types of Fluids Handled:** Centrifugal pumps can be employed for lifting highly viscous liquids, paper pulp, muddy and sewage water, oil, sugar molasses, etc. Reciprocating pumps are generally employed for light oil pumping, feeding small boilers, etc.
5.  **Cost and Maintenance:** The cost of a centrifugal pump is less as it has fewer parts. Its installation and maintenance are also easier and cheaper compared to a reciprocating pump.
6.  **Size and Weight:** For the same capacity and energy transfer, a centrifugal pump is compact and has a smaller size and weight than a reciprocating pump.
7.  **Speed of Operation:** Centrifugal pumps can be operated at very high speeds without any danger of separation and cavitation, allowing them to be directly coupled to electric motors or oil engines.
8.  **Efficiency:** The total efficiency of a reciprocating pump is about 10 to 20% higher than a comparable centrifugal pump. However, overall performance characteristics of centrifugal pumps are considered superior for most general applications.

### (b) How can we obtain an expression for the minimum speed for starting a centrifugal pump? Explain.

When a centrifugal pump is started, it will start delivering liquid only if the pressure rise in the impeller is more than or equal to the manometric head ($H_{mano}$). In other words, there will be no flow of liquid until the speed of the pump is such that the centrifugal head caused by the centrifugal force on the rotating water equals or exceeds the manometric head. 

The condition for flow to commence is:
$$\frac{u_2^2 - u_1^2}{2g} \ge H_{mano}$$

For the **minimum speed**, we equate the centrifugal head to the manometric head:
$$\frac{u_2^2 - u_1^2}{2g} = H_{mano} \quad \dots(1)$$

We also know that manometric efficiency ($\eta_{mano}$) is given by:
$$\eta_{mano} = \frac{gH_{mano}}{V_{w2}u_2}$$
$$\therefore H_{mano} = \eta_{mano} \times \left( \frac{V_{w2}u_2}{g} \right)$$

The tangential velocities at inlet and outlet are:
$$u_1 = \frac{\pi D_1 N}{60}; \quad u_2 = \frac{\pi D_2 N}{60}$$

Substituting the values of $H_{mano}$, $u_1$, and $u_2$ into equation (1), we get:
$$\frac{1}{2g} \left[ \left(\frac{\pi D_2 N}{60}\right)^2 - \left(\frac{\pi D_1 N}{60}\right)^2 \right] = \eta_{mano} \times \frac{V_{w2}}{g} \times \left(\frac{\pi D_2 N}{60}\right)$$

Dividing both sides by $\frac{\pi N}{g \times 60}$, we obtain:
$$\frac{\pi N}{120} (D_2^2 - D_1^2) = \eta_{mano} \times (V_{w2} \times D_2)$$

Rearranging for the minimum speed $N$ (i.e., $N_{min}$):
$$N_{min} = \frac{120 \times \eta_{mano} \times V_{w2} \times D_2}{\pi (D_2^2 - D_1^2)}$$

### Derive an expression of work done by the centrifugal pump on water.

The expression for work done or energy supplied by the impeller of a centrifugal pump on the liquid flowing through may be derived in a similar way as for a turbine.

Let,
$N$ = Speed of the impeller in r.p.m.
$\omega$ = Angular velocity $= \frac{2\pi N}{60}$ rad/s
$u_1$ = Tangential velocity of the impeller at inlet $= \frac{\pi D_1 N}{60} = \omega R_1$
$u_2$ = Tangential velocity of impeller at outlet $= \frac{\pi D_2 N}{60} = \omega R_2$
$V_1$ = Absolute velocity of water at inlet
$V_{w1}$ = Velocity of whirl at inlet
$V_{w2}$ = Velocity of whirl at outlet
$W$ = Weight of water delivered per second

**Assumptions:**
Liquid enters the impeller eye in a radial direction, which means the whirl component $V_{w1}$ (of the inlet absolute velocity $V_1$) is zero.

While passing through the impeller, the velocity of whirl changes, and there is a change of moment of momentum.
Torque on the impeller = Rate of change of moment of momentum
Moment of momentum at inlet = $0$ (since $V_{w1} = 0$)
Moment of momentum at outlet = $\frac{W}{g} (V_{w2} R_2)$

$\therefore$ Torque = $\frac{W}{g} (V_{w2} R_2)$

Work done per second = Torque $\times$ angular velocity
$= \frac{W}{g} (V_{w2} R_2) \times \omega = \frac{W}{g} (V_{w2} u_2)$   (Since $u_2 = \omega R_2$)

Work done per second per unit weight of liquid
$= \frac{V_{w2} u_2}{g}$

If the flow at inlet is not radial, the general expression for work done per second per unit weight of liquid is written as:
$= \frac{1}{g} (V_{w2} u_2 - V_{w1} u_1)$

### Differentiate between turbine and pump.

The basic difference between a turbine and a pump, from a hydrodynamic point of view, is that in a turbine, flow takes place from the high pressure side to the low pressure side, whereas in a pump, flow takes place from the low pressure towards the higher pressure. Thus, in a turbine, there is accelerated flow, while in a pump, the flow is decelerated. Furthermore, a turbine converts the energy of flowing water into mechanical energy, whereas a pump uses mechanical energy to provide energy to a fluid to increase its pressure or kinetic energy.

### Differentiate between the volute casing and vortex casing for the centrifugal pump.

*   **Volute casing:** In this type of casing, the area of flow gradually increases from the impeller outlet to the delivery pipe so as to reduce the velocity of flow. The decrease in velocity results in an increase in pressure (kinetic energy is converted into pressure energy) within the volute casing.
*   **Vortex casing:** If a circular chamber is provided between the impeller and the volute chamber, the casing is known as a vortex casing (or whirlpool chamber). The vortex chamber converts some of the kinetic energy into pressure energy before the fluid enters the volute chamber, which further increases the pressure energy. Thus, the efficiency of a volute pump fitted with a vortex chamber is higher than that of a simple volute pump.

Discharge, $Q = 200 \text{ lit/s} = 0.2 \text{ m}^3\text{/s}$
Total head (Manometric head), $H_{mano} = 20 \text{ m}$
Manometric efficiency, $\eta_{mano} = 80\% = 0.80$

**(a) Diameter of the impeller at outlet ($D_2$):**

From the outlet velocity triangle, we have:
$\tan \phi = \frac{V_{f2}}{u_2 - V_{w2}}$
$\tan 45^\circ = \frac{2.5}{u_2 - V_{w2}}$
Since $\tan 45^\circ = 1$:
$1 = \frac{2.5}{u_2 - V_{w2}}$
$V_{w2} = u_2 - 2.5$

Manometric efficiency is given by:
$\eta_{mano} = \frac{g H_{mano}}{V_{w2} u_2}$
$0.80 = \frac{9.81 \times 20}{V_{w2} \times u_2}$
$V_{w2} \times u_2 = \frac{196.2}{0.80} = 245.25$

Substitute $V_{w2} = u_2 - 2.5$ into the equation:
$(u_2 - 2.5) u_2 = 245.25$
$u_2^2 - 2.5 u_2 - 245.25 = 0$

Solving this quadratic equation for $u_2$:
$u_2 = \frac{2.5 \pm \sqrt{(-2.5)^2 - 4(1)(-245.25)}}{2(1)}$
$u_2 = \frac{2.5 \pm \sqrt{6.25 + 981}}{2}$
$u_2 = \frac{2.5 \pm \sqrt{987.25}}{2}$
$u_2 = \frac{2.5 \pm 31.42}{2}$
Taking the positive root:
$u_2 = \frac{33.92}{2} = 16.96 \text{ m/s}$

We know the tangential velocity is:
$u_2 = \frac{\pi D_2 N}{60}$
$16.96 = \frac{\pi \times D_2 \times 1000}{60}$
$D_2 = \frac{16.96 \times 60}{\pi \times 1000}$
$D_2 = \mathbf{0.3239 \text{ m or } 323.9 \text{ mm (Ans.)}}$

**(b) Width of the impeller at the outlet ($B_2$):**

Discharge is given by:
$Q = \pi D_2 B_2 V_{f2}$
$0.2 = \pi \times 0.3239 \times B_2 \times 2.5$
$0.2 = 2.5439 \times B_2$
$B_2 = \frac{0.2}{2.5439}$
$B_2 = \mathbf{0.0786 \text{ m or } 78.6 \text{ mm (Ans.)}}$
### Q3: A centrifugal pump impeller runs at 80 rpm and has an outlet vane angle of 60 degrees. The velocity of the flow is 2.5 m/s throughout, and the diameter of the impeller at the exit is twice that at the inlet. If the Manometric head is 20 m and the Manometric efficiency is 75 percent, determine i) the diameter of the impeller at the exit, and ii) the inlet vane angle.

**Solution:**

**Given Data:**
Speed, $N = 80 \text{ rpm}$
Outlet vane angle, $\phi = 60^\circ$
Velocity of flow, $V_{f1} = V_{f2} = 2.5 \text{ m/s}$
Manometric head, $H_{mano} = 20 \text{ m}$
Manometric efficiency, $\eta_{mano} = 75\% = 0.75$
Diameter of the impeller at outlet, $D_2 = 2D_1$ (diameter at inlet)

**i) The diameter of the impeller at the exit, $D_2$:**
Manometric efficiency is given by,
$$\eta_{mano} = \frac{g H_{mano}}{V_{w2} u_2}$$
$$0.75 = \frac{9.81 \times 20}{V_{w2} u_2}$$
$$V_{w2} u_2 = \frac{9.81 \times 20}{0.75} = 261.6 \quad \dots(1)$$

From velocity triangle at outlet, we have:
$$\tan \phi = \frac{V_{f2}}{u_2 - V_{w2}}$$
$$\tan 60^\circ = \frac{2.5}{u_2 - V_{w2}}$$
$$u_2 - V_{w2} = \frac{2.5}{\tan 60^\circ} = \frac{2.5}{1.732} = 1.44 \text{ m/s}$$
$$V_{w2} = u_2 - 1.44 \quad \dots(2)$$

Substituting this value of $V_{w2}$ in equation (1), we get:
$$(u_2 - 1.44) u_2 = 261.6$$
$$u_2^2 - 1.44 u_2 - 261.6 = 0$$

Solving the quadratic equation:
$$u_2 = \frac{1.44 \pm \sqrt{1.44^2 - 4(1)(-261.6)}}{2}$$
$$u_2 = \frac{1.44 \pm \sqrt{2.0736 + 1046.4}}{2}$$
$$u_2 = \frac{1.44 \pm 32.38}{2}$$
Taking the positive value, $u_2 = 16.91 \text{ m/s}$.

Also, tangential velocity of impeller at outlet is given by:
$$u_2 = \frac{\pi D_2 N}{60}$$
$$16.91 = \frac{\pi \times D_2 \times 80}{60}$$
$$D_2 = \frac{16.91 \times 60}{\pi \times 80} = \mathbf{4.037 \text{ m (Ans.)}}$$

**ii) Inlet vane angle, $\theta$:**
Tangential velocity of the impeller at inlet, $u_1$:
Since $D_2 = 2D_1$, then $u_1 = \frac{u_2}{2}$
$$u_1 = \frac{16.91}{2} = 8.455 \text{ m/s}$$

Assuming radial entry, from velocity triangle at inlet, we have:
$$\tan \theta = \frac{V_{f1}}{u_1}$$
$$\tan \theta = \frac{2.5}{8.455} = 0.2957$$
$$\theta = \tan^{-1}(0.2957) = \mathbf{16.47^\circ \text{ (Ans.)}}$$

### Q1: Enumerate the losses which occur when a centrifugal pump operates.

When a centrifugal pump operates, the various losses which occur are as follows:

1.  **Hydraulic losses:**
    (i) Hydraulic losses in the pump:
        (a) Shock or eddy losses at the entrance to and exit from the impeller.
        (b) Losses due to friction in the impeller.
        (c) Friction and eddy losses in the guide vanes/diffuser and casing.
    (ii) Other hydraulic losses:
        (a) Friction and other minor losses in the suction pipe.
        (b) Friction and other minor losses in the delivery pipe.
2.  **Mechanical losses:**
    (i) Losses due to disc friction between the impeller and the liquid which fills the clearance spaces between the impeller and casing.
    (ii) Losses pertaining to friction of the main bearing and glands.
3.  **Leakage loss:**
    The loss of energy due to leakage of liquid is known as leakage loss.
