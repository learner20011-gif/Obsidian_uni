## **Overview: Biot-Savart Law and Magnetic Field Intensity**

These examples utilize the Biot-Savart Law and the superposition principle to calculate the Magnetic Field Intensity ($\mathbf{H}$) generated by current-carrying conductors.

**Key Formulas used:**
1.  **Infinite Line Current:** $$ \mathbf{H} = \frac{I}{2\pi\rho} \mathbf{a}_\phi $$
2.  **Semi-Infinite Line Current (endpoint at origin):** $$ \mathbf{H} = \frac{I}{4\pi\rho} \mathbf{a}_\phi $$
3.  **Finite Line Segment:** $$ \mathbf{H} = \frac{I}{4\pi\rho} (\cos\alpha_2 - \cos\alpha_1) \mathbf{a}_\phi $$
4.  **Circular Loop (on axis):** $$ \mathbf{H} = \frac{I \rho^2}{2(\rho^2 + h^2)^{3/2}} \mathbf{a}_z $$

### **Example 7.2: A current filament carries a current $I = 3 \text{ A}$. The current flows along the positive $x$-axis toward the origin, and then up the positive $z$-axis away from the origin. Find $\mathbf{H}$ at $(-3, 4, 0)$ due to the current filament shown in Figure 7.7(a).**   
![[Pasted image 20260119193638.png]]
We apply the principle of **superposition**. The total magnetic field is the sum of the fields contributed by the two distinct sections of the wire:
$$ \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{H}_1 + \mathbf{H}_2 $$
Where:
*   $\mathbf{H}_1$ is the field due to the semi-infinite wire on the $x$-axis.
*   $\mathbf{H}_2$ is the field due to the semi-infinite wire on the $z$-axis.

#### **1. Analysis of $\mathbf{H}_2$ (The $z$-axis segment)**
This segment is a semi-infinite line end at the origin ($z=0$) and from $\infty$.

*   **Observation Point ($P$):** $(-3, 4, 0)$.
*   **Radial Distance ($\rho$):** The distance from the $z$-axis to $P$.
    $$ \rho = \sqrt{(-3)^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5 $$
*   **Angles:** Since the wire starts at the origin and goes to infinity:
    *   $\alpha_1 = 90^\circ$ (at the origin, relative to the vector to P).
    *   $\alpha_2 = 0^\circ$ (at infinity).
    *   Using the formula: $\mathbf{H} = \frac{I}{4\pi\rho} (\cos\alpha_2 - \cos\alpha_1)a_\phi$? The book simplifies this for a semi-infinite wire starting at the origin as just $\frac{I}{4\pi\rho}a_\phi$.
    *   Magnitude: $$ H_2 = \frac{3}{4\pi(5)} (1 - 0) = \frac{3}{20\pi} \text{ A/m} $$
*   **Direction ($\mathbf{a}_{\phi}$):**
    Using the cross product rule $\mathbf{a}_{\phi} = \mathbf{a}_l \times \mathbf{a}_\rho$.
    *   Current direction $\mathbf{a}_l = \mathbf{a}_z$.
    *   Radial vector from axis to point: $\mathbf{r} = -3\mathbf{a}_x + 4\mathbf{a}_y$.
    *   Unit radial vector $\mathbf{a}_\rho = \frac{-3\mathbf{a}_x + 4\mathbf{a}_y}{5}$.
    $$ \mathbf{a}_{\phi} = -\mathbf{a}_z \times \left( -\frac{3}{5}\mathbf{a}_x + \frac{4}{5}\mathbf{a}_y \right) = \frac{3}{5}(\mathbf{a}_z \times \mathbf{a}_x) - \frac{4}{5}(\mathbf{a}_z \times \mathbf{a}_y) $$
    $$ \mathbf{a}_{\phi} = \frac{3}{5}(\mathbf{a}_y) - \frac{4}{5}(-\mathbf{a}_x) = \frac{4}{5}\mathbf{a}_x +\frac{3}{5}\mathbf{a}_y $$
*   **Calculating $\mathbf{H}_2$:**
    $$ \mathbf{H}_2 = \frac{3}{20\pi} \left( \frac{4}{5}\mathbf{a}_x + \frac{3}{5}\mathbf{a}_y \right) $$
    $$ \mathbf{H}_2 = 38.2\mathbf{a}_x + 28.65\mathbf{a}_y \text{ mA/m} $$
 

#### **2. Analysis of $\mathbf{H}_1$ (The $x$-axis segment)**
This segment lies on the positive $x$-axis. The point $P(-3, 4, 0)$ is **not** at the "end" of this semi-infinite wire (which ends at $x=0$). The point has an $x$-coordinate of $-3$, meaning it is "behind" the termination point.

*   **Geometry:**
    *   Current flows along the $x$-axis from the origin.
    *   Distance from wire (which is on $x$-axis) to point $P(-3, 4, 0)$ is simply the distance in $y$ and $z$. Since $z=0$, distance is $y=4$. So $\rho = 4$.
*   **Angles:**
    *   Wire extends to $x = +\infty$ from $x = 0$. Point is at $x = -3$.
    *   $\alpha_2 = 0^\circ$ (at infinity).
    *   $\alpha_1$: Consider the triangle formed by the origin, the point $(-3,0,0)$ on the axis, and $P(-3,4,0)$.
    *   $\cos \alpha_1 = \frac{3}{\sqrt{3^2 + 4^2}} = \frac{3}{5}$.
*   **Direction:**
    *   Current is along $a_l=\mathbf{a}_x$ (towards origin)
    *   $\mathbf{a}_\phi =a_l \times a_p =\mathbf{a}_x \times \mathbf{a}_y = \mathbf{a}_z$.
*   **Calculating $\mathbf{H}_1$:**
   
    The text uses:
    $$ \mathbf{H}_1 = \frac{3}{4\pi(4)} \left(1 - \frac{3}{5}\right) \mathbf{a}_z $$
    $$ \mathbf{H}_1 = \frac{3}{16\pi} \left(\frac{2}{5}\right) \mathbf{a}_z = 23.87 \mathbf{a}_z \text{ mA/m} $$

#### **3. Total Field**
$$ \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{H}_1 + \mathbf{H}_2 = 38.2\mathbf{a}_x + 28.65\mathbf{a}_y + 23.87\mathbf{a}_z \text{ mA/m} $$

### **Practice Exercise 7.2: The positive $y$-axis (semi-infinite line with respect to the origin) carries a filamentary current of $2 \text{ A}$ in the $-\mathbf{a}_y$ direction. Find $\mathbf{H}$ at: (a) $A(2, 3, 0)$, (b) $B(3, 12, -4)$**


**Configuration:** Current flows from $+\infty$ down to the origin $(0,0,0)$ along the $y$-axis. Magnitude $I = 2 \text{ A}$.

#### **Part (a): Point $A(2, 3, 0)$**
*   **Radial Distance ($\rho$):** The perpendicular distance from the $y$-axis to point $(2,3,0)$ is the $x$-distance.
    $$ \rho = 2 \text{ m} $$
*   **Geometry Factor (Angles):**
    *   The wire exists for $y \in [0, \infty)$.
    *   Angle to $\infty$: $0^\circ \rightarrow \cos = 1$.
    *   Angle to Origin ($0,0,0$): The vertical distance is $3$, horizontal distance is $2$.
        $$ \cos \alpha = \frac{3}{\sqrt{2^2 + 3^2}} = \frac{3}{\sqrt{13}} $$
    *   Since the point ($y=3$) is between the start ($y=\infty$) and end ($y=0$) of the wire, the ==angles add up==.
    *   Magnitude Factor: $\left(1 + \frac{3}{\sqrt{13}}\right)$.
*   **Direction:**
    *   Current $\mathbf{I}$ is in $-\mathbf{a}_y$.
    *   Radial vector $\mathbf{a}_\rho$ is in $+\mathbf{a}_x$.
    *   Cross product: $(-\mathbf{a}_y) \times (\mathbf{a}_x) = \mathbf{a}_z$.
*   **Calculation:**
    $$ \mathbf{H} = \frac{I}{4\pi\rho} \left( 1 + \frac{3}{\sqrt{13}} \right) \mathbf{a}_z $$
    $$ \mathbf{H} = \frac{2}{4\pi(2)} \left( 1 + 0.832 \right) \mathbf{a}_z = \frac{1}{4\pi} (1.832) \mathbf{a}_z $$
    $$ \mathbf{H} \approx 0.1458 \mathbf{a}_z \text{ A/m} = \mathbf{145.8 \mathbf{a}_z \text{ mA/m}} $$

#### **Part (b): Point $B(3, 12, -4)$**
*   **Radial Distance ($\rho$):** Distance from $y$-axis. Point has $x=3, z=-4$.
    $$ \rho = \sqrt{3^2 + (-4)^2} = 5 \text{ m} $$
*   **Geometry Factor (Angles):**
    *   Wire: $y \in [0, \infty)$. Point is at $y=12$.
    *   Cos Angle to $\infty \rightarrow 1$.
    *   Angle to Origin: Y distance is $12$, and ($\rho$) is $5$.
    *   $\cos \alpha = \frac{12}{\sqrt{12^2 + 5^2}} = \frac{12}{13}$.
    *   Factor: $\left(1 + \frac{12}{13}\right)$. add up bcz point inside
*   **Direction:**
    *   Current: $-\mathbf{a}_y$.
    *   Vector from axis to point: $3\mathbf{a}_x - 4\mathbf{a}_z$.
    *   $\mathbf{a}_\rho = \frac{3\mathbf{a}_x - 4\mathbf{a}_z}{5}$.
    *   $\mathbf{a}_\phi = (-\mathbf{a}_y) \times \left( \frac{3\mathbf{a}_x - 4\mathbf{a}_z}{5} \right)$.
    *   $\mathbf{a}_\phi = \frac{1}{5} [ (- \mathbf{a}_y \times 3\mathbf{a}_x) - (-\mathbf{a}_y \times 4\mathbf{a}_z) ]$.
    *   $\mathbf{a}_\phi =\frac{1}{5} [ 3(\mathbf{a}_z) + 4(\mathbf{a}_x) ]  = 0.8\mathbf{a}_x + 0.6\mathbf{a}_z$.
*   **Calculation:**
    $$ H_{mag} = \frac{2}{4\pi(5)} \left( 1 + \frac{12}{13} \right) = \frac{1}{10\pi} \left( \frac{25}{13} \right) \approx 0.0612 \text{ A/m} $$
    $$ \mathbf{H} = 61.2 \text{ mA/m} \times (0.8\mathbf{a}_x + 0.6\mathbf{a}_z) $$
    $$ \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{48.97 \mathbf{a}_x + 36.73 \mathbf{a}_z \text{ mA/m}} $$

### **Example 7.3: A circular loop located on $x^2 + y^2 = 9, z=0$ carries a direct current of $10 \text{ A}$ along $\mathbf{a}_\phi$. Determine $\mathbf{H}$ at $(0, 0, 4)$ and $(0, 0, -4)$.**

![[Pasted image 20260119193619.png]]
This example derives the general formula for a circular loop and then applies it.

#### **1. Derivation of the Formula**
Using the Biot-Savart Law:
$$ d\mathbf{H} = \frac{I d\mathbf{l} \times \mathbf{R}}{4\pi |R|^3} $$

*   **Source ($dl$):** Located on the ring at radius $\rho$.
    $$ d\mathbf{l} = \rho \, d\phi \, \mathbf{a}_\phi $$
*   **Vector $\mathbf{R}$:** From source point $(\rho, \phi, 0)$ to observation point $(0, 0, h)$.
- **Target Point:** $(0, 0, h)$ (On the $z$-axis).
- **Source Point:** $(x, y, 0)$ (On the loop).
1. Cartesian Difference: $$\mathbf{R} = (0-x)\mathbf{a}_x + (0-y)\mathbf{a}_y + (h-0)\mathbf{a}_z = -x\mathbf{a}_x - y\mathbf{a}_y + h\mathbf{a}_z$$
    
2. **Cylindrical Conversion:**
    
    - Radial distance: $\rho = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$
        
    - Directional mapping: $-(x\mathbf{a}_x + y\mathbf{a}_y) = -\rho \mathbf{a}_\rho$
    
$$\mathbf{R} = -\rho \mathbf{a}_\rho + h \mathbf{a}_z$$
*   **Cross Product ($d\mathbf{l} \times \mathbf{R}$):**
    $$ (\rho \, d\phi \, \mathbf{a}_\phi) \times (-\rho \mathbf{a}_\rho + h \mathbf{a}_z) $$
$$ = -\rho^2 \, d\phi (\mathbf{a}_\phi \times \mathbf{a}_\rho) + \rho h \, d\phi (\mathbf{a}_\phi \times \mathbf{a}_z) $$
$$ = \rho h \, d\phi \, \mathbf{a}_\rho + \rho^2 \, d\phi \, \mathbf{a}_z $$
    *(Note: $\mathbf{a}_\phi \times \mathbf{a}_\rho = -\mathbf{a}_z$. The text simplifies the vector components differently, arguing by symmetry).
*   **Symmetry Argument:**
    The radial components ($\mathbf{a}_\rho$) cancel out when integrated around the whole circle (0 to $2\pi$). Only the $\mathbf{a}_z$ component survives.
*   **Integration:**
    $$ \mathbf{H} = \int_0^{2\pi} \frac{I \rho^2 d\phi \mathbf{a}_z}{4\pi (\rho^2 + h^2)^{3/2}} $$
    $$ \mathbf{H} = \frac{I \rho^2 (2\pi) \mathbf{a}_z}{4\pi (\rho^2 + h^2)^{3/2}} $$
    **Final Formula:**
    $$ \mathbf{H} = \frac{I \rho^2}{2(\rho^2 + h^2)^{3/2}} \mathbf{a}_z $$

#### **2. Application to Problem**
*   **Given:**
    *   Current $I = 10 \text{ A}$.
    *   Loop Radius $\rho = \sqrt{9} = 3 \text{ m}$.
    *   Height $h = 4 \text{ m}$.
*   **At $(0, 0, 4)$:**
    $$ \mathbf{H} = \frac{10 (3^2)}{2(3^2 + 4^2)^{3/2}} \mathbf{a}_z $$
    $$ \mathbf{H} = \frac{90}{2(9 + 16)^{3/2}} \mathbf{a}_z = \frac{45}{(25)^{3/2}} \mathbf{a}_z = \frac{45}{125} \mathbf{a}_z $$
    $$ \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{0.36 \mathbf{a}_z \text{ A/m}} $$
*   **At $(0, 0, -4)$:**
    Due to symmetry, the field direction and magnitude on the axis depend on the square of $h$   ($h^2$). Therefore, the result is identical.
    $$ \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{0.36 \mathbf{a}_z \text{ A/m}} $$

### **Practice Exercise 7.3: A thin ring of radius $5 \text{ cm}$ is placed on plane $z = 1 \text{ cm}$ so that its center is at $(0, 0, 1 \text{ cm})$. If the ring carries $50 \text{ mA}$ along $\mathbf{a}_\phi$, find $\mathbf{H}$ at: (a) $(0, 0, -1 \text{ cm})$, (b) $(0, 0, 10 \text{ cm})$**


This problem utilizes the formula derived in Example 7.3, but we must carefully determine $h$, which is the distance from the center of the ring to the observation point.

**Constants:**
*   Radius $\rho = 5 \text{ cm} = 0.05 \text{ m}$.
*   Current $I = 50 \text{ mA} = 0.05 \text{ A}$.
*   Center of ring $z_{center} = 1 \text{ cm} = 0.01 \text{ m}$.

**Formula:**
$$ \mathbf{H} = \frac{I \rho^2}{2(\rho^2 + h^2)^{3/2}} \mathbf{a}_z $$

#### **Part (a): Point $(0, 0, -1 \text{ cm})$**
*   **Calculate $h$:**
    The point is at $z = -1 \text{ cm}$. The ring is at $z = 1 \text{ cm}$.
    $$ h = |1 - (-1)| = 2 \text{ cm} = 0.02 \text{ m} $$
*   **Substitute Values:**
    *   Numerator: $I \rho^2 = 0.05 \times (0.05)^2 = 0.05 \times 0.0025 = 1.25 \times 10^{-4}$.
    *   Denominator term $(\rho^2 + h^2)$: $(0.05)^2 + (0.02)^2 = 0.0025 + 0.0004 = 0.0029$.
    *   Denominator: $2 \times (0.0029)^{1.5}$.
*   **Calculation:**
    $$ \mathbf{H} = \frac{1.25 \times 10^{-4}}{2 \times (0.0029)^{1.5}} \mathbf{a}_z $$
    $$ (0.0029)^{1.5} \approx 1.56 \times 10^{-4} $$
    $$ \mathbf{H} \approx \frac{1.25 \times 10^{-4}}{3.12 \times 10^{-4}} \mathbf{a}_z \approx 0.4 \mathbf{a}_z \text{ A/m} $$
*   **Convert to mA/m:**
    $$ \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{400 \mathbf{a}_z \text{ mA/m}} $$

#### **Part (b): Point $(0, 0, 10 \text{ cm})$**
*   **Calculate $h$:**
    The point is at $z = 10 \text{ cm}$. The ring is at $z = 1 \text{ cm}$.
    $$ h = |10 - 1| = 9 \text{ cm} = 0.09 \text{ m} $$
*   **Substitute Values:**
    *   Numerator: $1.25 \times 10^{-4}$ (same as above).
    *   Denominator term $(\rho^2 + h^2)$: $(0.05)^2 + (0.09)^2 = 0.0025 + 0.0081 = 0.0106$.
    *   Denominator: $2 \times (0.0106)^{1.5}$.
*   **Calculation:**
    $$ (0.0106)^{1.5} \approx 0.00109 $$
    $$ \mathbf{H} = \frac{1.25 \times 10^{-4}}{2 \times 0.00109} \mathbf{a}_z = \frac{1.25 \times 10^{-4}}{0.00218} \mathbf{a}_z $$
    $$ \mathbf{H} \approx 0.0573 \mathbf{a}_z \text{ A/m} $$
*   **Convert to mA/m:**
    $$ \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{57.3 \mathbf{a}_z \text{ mA/m}} $$  
## **Section 2: Magnetic Flux Density (B) & Vector Potential (A)**

### **Example 6-4: A direct current $I$ flows in a straight wire of length $2L$. Find the magnetic flux density $\mathbf{B}$ at a point located at a distance $r$ from the wire in the bisecting plane: (a) by determining the vector magnetic potential $\mathbf{A}$ first, and (b) by applying the Biot-Savart law.**

**Context:**
*   The wire is aligned with the $z$-axis.
*   The wire extends from $z = -L$ to $z = +L$.
*   The observation point $P$ is at cylindrical coordinates $(r, 0, 0)$.
*   Current flows in the $+\mathbf{a}_z$ direction.

---

#### **Solution Part (a): Using Vector Magnetic Potential $\mathbf{A}$**

This method involves finding the potential field $\mathbf{A}$ first, then performing a curl operation ($\nabla \times \mathbf{A}$) to find the magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$.

**1. Define the Differential Element:**
*   The current element is $d\mathbf{l}' = dz' \mathbf{a}_z$.
*   The distance $R$ from a source element at $z'$ to the point $P$ at $r$ is the hypotenuse of the triangle formed by $r$ and $z'$:
    $$ R = \sqrt{z'^2 + r^2} $$

**2. Setup the Integral for $\mathbf{A}$:**
The general formula for vector potential is:
$$ \mathbf{A} = \frac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi} \oint \frac{d\mathbf{l}'}{R} $$
Substituting our specific geometry:
$$ \mathbf{A} = \mathbf{a}_z \frac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi} \int_{-L}^{L} \frac{dz'}{\sqrt{z'^2 + r^2}} $$

**3. Evaluate the Integral:**
Using the standard integral form $\int \frac{dx}{\sqrt{x^2 + a^2}} = \ln(x + \sqrt{x^2 + a^2})$:
$$ \mathbf{A} = \mathbf{a}_z \frac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi} \left[ \ln(z' + \sqrt{z'^2 + r^2}) \right]_{-L}^{L} $$
Applying the limits ($L$ and $-L$) and simplifying using logarithm rules ($\ln A - \ln B = \ln(A/B)$):
$$ \mathbf{A} = \mathbf{a}_z \frac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi} \ln \left[ \frac{\sqrt{L^2 + r^2} + L}{\sqrt{L^2 + r^2} - L} \right] $$

**4. Calculate $\mathbf{B}$ using Curl ($\nabla \times \mathbf{A}$):**
In cylindrical coordinates, the curl of $\mathbf{A}$ is:
$$ \mathbf{B} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A} = \left( \frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial A_z}{\partial \phi} - \frac{\partial A_\phi}{\partial z} \right)\mathbf{a}_r + \left( \frac{\partial A_r}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial A_z}{\partial r} \right)\mathbf{a}_\phi + \frac{1}{r}\left( \frac{\partial (r A_\phi)}{\partial r} - \frac{\partial A_r}{\partial \phi} \right)\mathbf{a}_z $$

*   Since $\mathbf{A}$ only has a $z$-component ($A_z$), and due to cylindrical symmetry ($A_z$ does not change with $\phi$), the only surviving term is:
    $$ \mathbf{B} = - \frac{\partial A_z}{\partial r} \mathbf{a}_\phi $$

**5. Perform the Differentiation:**
Differentiating the logarithmic expression with respect to $r$ yields:
$$ \frac{\partial A_z}{\partial r} = \frac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi} \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \ln \left( \frac{\sqrt{L^2 + r^2} + L}{\sqrt{L^2 + r^2} - L} \right) $$
After performing the derivative algebra (chain rule):
$$ \mathbf{B} = \frac{\mu_0 I L}{2\pi r \sqrt{L^2 + r^2}} \mathbf{a}_\phi \quad \text{(Eq. 6-35)} $$

**Limiting Case (Infinite Wire):**
If the wire is infinitely long ($L \gg r$), then $\sqrt{L^2 + r^2} \approx L$. The equation simplifies to the standard Biot-Savart result for an infinite line:
$$ \mathbf{B} = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r} \mathbf{a}_\phi $$

---

#### **Solution Part (b): Using Biot-Savart Law**

This method calculates $\mathbf{B}$ directly by integrating the differential contributions $d\mathbf{B}$.

**1. Define Vectors:**
*   Source vector (position of current element): $\mathbf{r}' = z' \mathbf{a}_z$
*   Field vector (position of point $P$): $\mathbf{r}_p = r \mathbf{a}_r$
*   Distance vector $\mathbf{R} = \mathbf{r}_p - \mathbf{r}' = r \mathbf{a}_r - z' \mathbf{a}_z$
*   Magnitude $R = \sqrt{r^2 + z'^2}$

**2. The Cross Product ($d\mathbf{l}' \times \mathbf{R}$):**
$$ d\mathbf{l}' = dz' \mathbf{a}_z $$
$$ d\mathbf{l}' \times \mathbf{R} = (dz' \mathbf{a}_z) \times (r \mathbf{a}_r - z' \mathbf{a}_z) $$
Using cross product rules ($\mathbf{a}_z \times \mathbf{a}_r = \mathbf{a}_\phi$ and $\mathbf{a}_z \times \mathbf{a}_z = 0$):
$$ d\mathbf{l}' \times \mathbf{R} = r \, dz' \, \mathbf{a}_\phi $$

**3. Setup the Integral:**
$$ \mathbf{B} = \int_{-L}^{L} \frac{\mu_0 I (r \, dz')}{4\pi (z'^2 + r^2)^{3/2}} \mathbf{a}_\phi $$

**4. Solve the Integral:**
Using standard integral tables or trigonometric substitution ($z' = r \tan \theta$):
$$ \int \frac{dz'}{(z'^2 + r^2)^{3/2}} = \frac{z'}{r^2 \sqrt{z'^2 + r^2}} $$
Evaluating from $-L$ to $L$:
$$ \mathbf{B} = \frac{\mu_0 I r}{4\pi} \left[ \frac{z'}{r^2 \sqrt{z'^2 + r^2}} \right]_{-L}^{L} \mathbf{a}_\phi $$
$$ \mathbf{B} = \frac{\mu_0 I r}{4\pi r^2} \left( \frac{L}{\sqrt{L^2 + r^2}} - \frac{-L}{\sqrt{L^2 + r^2}} \right) \mathbf{a}_\phi $$
$$ \mathbf{B} = \frac{\mu_0 I L}{2\pi r \sqrt{L^2 + r^2}} \mathbf{a}_\phi $$

*This result matches exactly with the result found in Part (a).*

### **Example 6-5: Find the magnetic flux density at the center of a square loop, with side $w$ carrying a direct current $I$.**

**Context:**
*   A square loop lies in the $xy$-plane.
*   Side length is $w$.
*   We need the field at the geometric center.

#### **Solution**

**1. Principle of Superposition:**
The total magnetic field is the sum of the fields produced by the four individual sides of the square.
$$ \mathbf{B}_{total} = \mathbf{B}_{side1} + \mathbf{B}_{side2} + \mathbf{B}_{side3} + \mathbf{B}_{side4} $$
By symmetry, the magnitude of the field from each side is identical, and they all point in the same direction (along the $z$-axis, assuming counter-clockwise current). Thus:
$$ \mathbf{B}_{total} = 4 \times \mathbf{B}_{one\_side} $$

**2. Adapt Formula from Example 6-4:**
We treat one side of the square as a finite straight wire of length $2L = w$.
*   Length of wire ($2L$): $w \implies L = w/2$.
*   Distance from wire to center ($r$): $w/2$.

Substitute these into Eq. 6-35 ($\mathbf{B} = \frac{\mu_0 I L}{2\pi r \sqrt{L^2 + r^2}}$):

$$ B_{one\_side} = \frac{\mu_0 I (w/2)}{2\pi (w/2) \sqrt{(w/2)^2 + (w/2)^2}} $$

**3. Simplify:**
$$ B_{one\_side} = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi \sqrt{2(w/2)^2}} = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi (w/2)\sqrt{2}} = \frac{\mu_0 I}{\pi w \sqrt{2}} $$
Rationalizing the denominator (multiplying numerator and denominator by $\sqrt{2}$):
$$ B_{one\_side} = \frac{\sqrt{2}\mu_0 I}{2\pi w} $$

**4. Calculate Total Field:**
Multiply by 4 sides:
$$ \mathbf{B}_{total} = 4 \times \left( \frac{\sqrt{2}\mu_0 I}{2\pi w} \right) \mathbf{a}_z $$
$$ \mathbf{B}_{total} = \frac{2\sqrt{2}\mu_0 I}{\pi w} \mathbf{a}_z $$

### **Example 6-6: Find the magnetic flux density at a point on the axis of a circular loop of radius $b$ that carries a direct current $I$.**

**Context:**
*   Loop is in the $xy$-plane, centered at the origin.
*   Radius is $b$.
*   Current $I$ flows in the $\mathbf{a}_\phi$ direction.
*   Observation point $P$ is at $(0, 0, z)$.

#### **Solution**

We apply the Biot-Savart Law directly.

**1. Differential Element ($d\mathbf{l}'$):**
A small segment of the loop at radius $b$:
$$ d\mathbf{l}' = b \, d\phi' \, \mathbf{a}_\phi $$

**2. Distance Vector ($\mathbf{R}$):**
*   From source $(b, \phi', 0)$ to point $(0, 0, z)$:
*   $\mathbf{R} = z \mathbf{a}_z - b \mathbf{a}_\rho$ (Note: $\mathbf{a}_\rho$ is the radial unit vector in the cylindrical plane).
*   Magnitude: $R = \sqrt{z^2 + b^2}$.

**3. Cross Product ($d\mathbf{l}' \times \mathbf{R}$):**
$$ d\mathbf{l}' \times \mathbf{R} = (b \, d\phi' \, \mathbf{a}_\phi) \times (z \mathbf{a}_z - b \mathbf{a}_\rho) $$
Distributing the cross product:
1.  $\mathbf{a}_\phi \times \mathbf{a}_z = \mathbf{a}_\rho$
2.  $\mathbf{a}_\phi \times \mathbf{a}_\rho = -\mathbf{a}_z$

$$ d\mathbf{l}' \times \mathbf{R} = (b z \, d\phi') \mathbf{a}_\rho + (b^2 \, d\phi') \mathbf{a}_z $$

**4. Symmetry Argument:**
*   The term with $\mathbf{a}_\rho$ represents the radial component of the field.
*   For every element on the loop at angle $\phi$, there is a diametrically opposite element at $\phi + \pi$. Their radial contributions cancel out.
*   Therefore, we only integrate the $\mathbf{a}_z$ component.

**5. Perform the Integration:**
$$ \mathbf{B} = \int_{0}^{2\pi} \frac{\mu_0 I (b^2 \, d\phi')}{4\pi (z^2 + b^2)^{3/2}} \mathbf{a}_z $$

Since $I, b, z$ are all constant with respect to $\phi'$, they move outside the integral:
$$ \mathbf{B} = \frac{\mu_0 I b^2}{4\pi (z^2 + b^2)^{3/2}} \mathbf{a}_z \int_{0}^{2\pi} d\phi' $$
$$ \mathbf{B} = \frac{\mu_0 I b^2}{4\pi (z^2 + b^2)^{3/2}} (2\pi) \mathbf{a}_z $$

**6. Final Result:**
$$ \mathbf{B} = \frac{\mu_0 I b^2}{2(z^2 + b^2)^{3/2}} \mathbf{a}_z $$

**Special Case (Center of the loop):**
If $z = 0$, the formula simplifies to:$$ \mathbf{B} = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2b} \mathbf{a}_z $$v

### **Example 7.5: Planes $z=0$ and $z=4$ carry current $\mathbf{K} = -10\mathbf{a}_x$ A/m and $\mathbf{K} = 10\mathbf{a}_x$ A/m, respectively. Determine $\mathbf{H}$ at (a) $(1, 1, 1)$ and (b) $(0, -3, 10)$.**

*   **Principle: Magnetic Field of an Infinite Current Sheet**
    The magnetic field intensity $\mathbf{H}$ due to an infinite sheet of current density $\mathbf{K}$ is given by:
    $$ \mathbf{H} = \frac{1}{2} \mathbf{K} \times \mathbf{a}_n $$
    Where:
    *   $\mathbf{K}$ is the surface current density vector.
    *   $\mathbf{a}_n$ is the unit normal vector pointing from the current sheet *toward* the observation point.
    *   The total field is found using the **Principle of Superposition**: $\mathbf{H}_{total} = \mathbf{H}_{z=0} + \mathbf{H}_{z=4}$.

*   **Part (a): At point $(1, 1, 1)$**
    This point is located at $z=1$, which is **between** the two plates ($0 < 1 < 4$).

    *   **Contribution from sheet at $z=0$ ($\mathbf{H}_o$):**
        *   Current $\mathbf{K} = -10\mathbf{a}_x$.
        *   The point ($z=1$) is above the sheet ($z=0$), so the normal vector $\mathbf{a}_n = \mathbf{a}_z$.
        *   Calculation:
            $$ \mathbf{H}_o = \frac{1}{2}(-10\mathbf{a}_x) \times \mathbf{a}_z = -5(\mathbf{a}_x \times \mathbf{a}_z) $$
            $$ \mathbf{H}_o = -5(-\mathbf{a}_y) = 5\mathbf{a}_y \text{ A/m} $$

    *   **Contribution from sheet at $z=4$ ($\mathbf{H}_4$):**
        *   Current $\mathbf{K} = 10\mathbf{a}_x$.
        *   The point ($z=1$) is below the sheet ($z=4$), so the normal vector $\mathbf{a}_n = -\mathbf{a}_z$.
        *   Calculation:
            $$ \mathbf{H}_4 = \frac{1}{2}(10\mathbf{a}_x) \times (-\mathbf{a}_z) = -5(\mathbf{a}_x \times \mathbf{a}_z) $$
            $$ \mathbf{H}_4 = -5(-\mathbf{a}_y) = 5\mathbf{a}_y \text{ A/m} $$

    *   **Total Field:**
        $$ \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{H}_o + \mathbf{H}_4 = 5\mathbf{a}_y + 5\mathbf{a}_y = \mathbf{10\mathbf{a}_y \text{ A/m}} $$

*   **Part (b): At point $(0, -3, 10)$**
    This point is located at $z=10$, which is **above both** plates ($z > 4 > 0$).

    *   **Contribution from sheet at $z=0$ ($\mathbf{H}_o$):**
        *   Point is above the sheet, so $\mathbf{a}_n = \mathbf{a}_z$.
        *   Calculation:
            $$ \mathbf{H}_o = \frac{1}{2}(-10\mathbf{a}_x) \times \mathbf{a}_z = 5\mathbf{a}_y \text{ A/m} $$

            $$ \mathbf{H}_4 = 5(-\mathbf{a}_y) = -5\mathbf{a}_y \text{ A/m} $$

        $$ \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{H}_o + \mathbf{H}_4 = 5\mathbf{a}_y - 5\mathbf{a}_y = \mathbf{0 \text{ A/m}} $$

## **Section 4: Ampère's Circuit Law & Applications**

### **Example 7.6: A toroid whose dimensions are shown in Figure 7.15 has $N$ turns and carries current $I$. Determine $H$ inside and outside the toroid.**

*   **Principle: Ampère’s Circuit Law**
    $$ \oint \mathbf{H} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = I_{enc} $$
    The toroid exhibits symmetry such that $\mathbf{H}$ is constant along a circular path of radius $\rho$ centered at the origin.

*   **Region 1: Inside the Toroid core ($\rho_o - a < \rho < \rho_o + a$)**
    *   **Amperian Path:** A circle of radius $\rho$ inside the toroid.
    *   **Left Hand Side (LHS):** The integral of $H$ along the circular path of length $2\pi\rho$.
        $$ \oint \mathbf{H} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = H(2\pi\rho) $$
    *   **Right Hand Side (RHS) $I_{enc}$:** The path encloses the inner wires of the toroid. Since there are $N$ turns, and each carries current $I$, the total enclosed current is:
        $$ I_{enc} = NI $$
    *   **Result:**
        $$ H(2\pi\rho) = NI \implies H = \frac{NI}{2\pi\rho} $$
    *   **Vector Form:** The field lines follow the circular path ($\mathbf{a}_\phi$).
        $$ \mathbf{H} = \frac{NI}{2\pi\rho} \mathbf{a}_\phi $$

*   **Region 2: Outside the Toroid ($\rho < \rho_o - a$ or $\rho > \rho_o + a$)**
    *   **Inner Hole:** An Amperian path smaller than the inner radius encloses no current ($I_{enc} = 0$). Thus, $H=0$.
    *   **Exterior:** An Amperian path surrounding the entire toroid encloses $N$ currents going "up" and $N$ currents going "down" (or "in" and "out"). The net current is zero ($NI - NI = 0$).
    *   **Result:**
        $$ \mathbf{H} = 0 $$

*   **Approximation:**
    If the toroid is thin ($\rho \approx \rho_o$), the field is approximately uniform:
    $$ H_{approx} = \frac{NI}{2\pi\rho_o} = \frac{NI}{\ell} $$

### **Example 6-1: An infinitely long, straight conductor with a circular cross section of radius $b$ carries a steady current $I$. Determine the magnetic flux density both inside and outside the conductor.**

*   **Principle: Ampère’s Circuital Law**
    This problem possesses cylindrical symmetry. We align the conductor along the $z$-axis. The magnetic flux density $\mathbf{B}$ is directed tangentially ($\mathbf{a}_\phi$) and is constant along a circular path centered on the axis.
    $$ \oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \mu_0 I_{enc} $$

*   **Part A: Inside the conductor ($r_1 \leq b$)**
    *   **Amperian Path:** A circle of radius $r_1$ inside the conductor.
    *   **Left Hand Side (Integration):**
        $$ \oint \mathbf{B}_1 \cdot d\mathbf{l} = B_{\phi 1} (2\pi r_1) $$
    *   **Right Hand Side (Current Enclosed):**
        The current is distributed uniformly over the cross-sectional area $\pi b^2$. The current enclosed by the smaller circle (area $\pi r_1^2$) is a fraction of the total current:
        $$ I_{enc} = I \frac{\text{Area enclosed}}{\text{Total Area}} = I \frac{\pi r_1^2}{\pi b^2} = I \left( \frac{r_1}{b} \right)^2 $$
    *   **Solving for $\mathbf{B}_1$:**
        $$ B_{\phi 1} (2\pi r_1) = \mu_0 I \frac{r_1^2}{b^2} $$
        $$ B_{\phi 1} = \frac{\mu_0 I r_1}{2\pi b^2} $$
    *   **Vector Form:**
        $$ \mathbf{B}_1 = \frac{\mu_0 I r_1}{2\pi b^2} \mathbf{a}_\phi $$

*   **Part B: Outside the conductor ($r_2 \geq b$)**
    *   **Amperian Path:** A circle of radius $r_2$ outside the conductor.
    *   **Left Hand Side (Integration):**
        $$ \oint \mathbf{B}_2 \cdot d\mathbf{l} = B_{\phi 2} (2\pi r_2) $$
    *   **Right Hand Side (Current Enclosed):**
        The path encloses the entire conductor, so the total current is $I$.
        $$ I_{enc} = I $$
    *   **Solving for $\mathbf{B}_2$:**
        $$ B_{\phi 2} (2\pi r_2) = \mu_0 I $$
        $$ B_{\phi 2} = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r_2} $$
    *   **Vector Form:**
        $$ \mathbf{B}_2 = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r_2} \mathbf{a}_\phi $$

*   **Summary:**
    *   Inside, $\mathbf{B}$ increases linearly with distance from the center ($B \propto r$).
    *   Outside, $\mathbf{B}$ decreases inversely with distance ($B \propto 1/r$).
    *   At the surface ($r=b$), both formulas yield $\frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi b}$, ensuring continuity.

### **7.1 (a) State Biot–Savart’s law. (b) The $y$- and $z$-axes, respectively, carry filamentary currents $10$ A along $\mathbf{a}_y$ and $20$ A along $-\mathbf{a}_z$. Find $\mathbf{H}$ at $(-3, 4, 5)$.**

*   **Part (a): Biot-Savart's Law Statement**
    The differential magnetic field intensity $d\mathbf{H}$ produced at a point $P$ by a differential current element $I d\mathbf{l}$ is proportional to the product of $I d\mathbf{l}$ and the sine of the angle between the element and the line joining them, and inversely proportional to the square of the distance $R$ between them.
    $$ d\mathbf{H} = \frac{I d\mathbf{l} \times \mathbf{a}_R}{4\pi R^2} = \frac{I d\mathbf{l} \times \mathbf{R}}{4\pi R^3} $$

*   **Part (b): Calculation**
    We use the formula for the magnetic field of an infinitely long straight wire:
    $$ \mathbf{H} = \frac{I}{2\pi \rho} (\mathbf{a}_L \times \mathbf{a}_\rho) = \frac{I}{2\pi \rho^2} (\mathbf{a}_L \times \mathbf{r}_{perp}) $$
    Where $\mathbf{r}_{perp}$ is the vector from the wire to the point, and $\rho$ is the magnitude of that vector.

    **1. Field due to current on $y$-axis ($\mathbf{H}_y$):**
    *   Current $\mathbf{I} = 10 \mathbf{a}_y$.
    *   Point $P = (-3, 4, 5)$.
    *   The perpendicular vector from the $y$-axis (where $x=0, z=0$) to $P$ depends only on $x$ and $z$ coordinates:
        $$ \mathbf{r}_{y} = -3\mathbf{a}_x + 5\mathbf{a}_z $$
    *   Distance squared $\rho_y^2 = (-3)^2 + 5^2 = 9 + 25 = 34$.
    *   Calculate Cross Product $(\mathbf{a}_L \times \mathbf{r}_{y})$:
        $$ \mathbf{a}_y \times (-3\mathbf{a}_x + 5\mathbf{a}_z) = -3(\mathbf{a}_y \times \mathbf{a}_x) + 5(\mathbf{a}_y \times \mathbf{a}_z) $$
        $$ = -3(-\mathbf{a}_z) + 5(\mathbf{a}_x) = 5\mathbf{a}_x + 3\mathbf{a}_z $$
    *   Calculate $\mathbf{H}_y$:
        $$ \mathbf{H}_y = \frac{10}{2\pi(34)} (5\mathbf{a}_x + 3\mathbf{a}_z) = \frac{5}{34\pi} (5\mathbf{a}_x + 3\mathbf{a}_z) \approx 0.0468 (5\mathbf{a}_x + 3\mathbf{a}_z) $$
        $$ \mathbf{H}_y \approx 0.234\mathbf{a}_x + 0.140\mathbf{a}_z \text{ A/m} $$

    **2. Field due to current on $z$-axis ($\mathbf{H}_z$):**
    *   Current $\mathbf{I} = 20 (-\mathbf{a}_z) = -20 \mathbf{a}_z$.
    *   Point $P = (-3, 4, 5)$.
    *   The perpendicular vector from the $z$-axis to $P$ depends only on $x$ and $y$:
        $$ \mathbf{r}_{z} = -3\mathbf{a}_x + 4\mathbf{a}_y $$
    *   Distance squared $\rho_z^2 = (-3)^2 + 4^2 = 9 + 16 = 25$.
    *   Calculate Cross Product $(\mathbf{a}_L \times \mathbf{r}_{z})$:
        $$ (-\mathbf{a}_z) \times (-3\mathbf{a}_x + 4\mathbf{a}_y) = 3(\mathbf{a}_z \times \mathbf{a}_x) - 4(\mathbf{a}_z \times \mathbf{a}_y) $$
        $$ = 3(\mathbf{a}_y) - 4(-\mathbf{a}_x) = 4\mathbf{a}_x + 3\mathbf{a}_y $$
    *   Calculate $\mathbf{H}_z$:
        $$ \mathbf{H}_z = \frac{20}{2\pi(25)} (4\mathbf{a}_x + 3\mathbf{a}_y) = \frac{2}{5\pi} (4\mathbf{a}_x + 3\mathbf{a}_y) \approx 0.127 (4\mathbf{a}_x + 3\mathbf{a}_y) $$
        $$ \mathbf{H}_z \approx 0.509\mathbf{a}_x + 0.382\mathbf{a}_y \text{ A/m} $$

    **3. Total Field:**
    $$ \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{H}_y + \mathbf{H}_z $$
    $$ \mathbf{H} = (0.234 + 0.509)\mathbf{a}_x + (0.382)\mathbf{a}_y + (0.140)\mathbf{a}_z $$
    $$ \mathbf{H} = 0.743\mathbf{a}_x + 0.382\mathbf{a}_y + 0.140\mathbf{a}_z \text{ A/m} $$
    *(Or in Cartesian form: $\mathbf{H} = 236.5\mathbf{a}_x + 382\mathbf{a}_y + 140.5\mathbf{a}_z$ mA/m)*

    *   **Surface Definition:** The surface lies in the plane $\phi = 90^\circ$ (the $yz$-plane). The unit normal vector to this surface is $\mathbf{a}_\phi$ (specifically $\mathbf{a}_\phi$ at $90^\circ$ corresponds to $-\mathbf{a}_x$, but for flux direction relative to the $\mathbf{a}_\phi$ field, we simply use the dot product).
    *   **Field Orientation:** The magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ circles the $z$-axis in the $\mathbf{a}_\phi$ direction. Since the surface is defined by constant $\phi$, the field lines pass perpendicularly through the surface.
    *   **Flux Integral:**
        $$ \Phi = \int_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{S} $$
        $$ d\mathbf{S} = d\rho \, dz \, \mathbf{a}_\phi $$
        $$ \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \left( \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi \rho} \mathbf{a}_\phi \right) \cdot (d\rho \, dz \, \mathbf{a}_\phi) = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi \rho} d\rho \, dz $$
    *   **Calculation:**
        $$ \Phi = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi} \int_{z=0}^{4} \int_{\rho=2}^{6} \frac{1}{\rho} d\rho \, dz $$
        $$ \Phi = \frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7})(2)}{2\pi} [z]_0^4 [\ln \rho]_2^6 $$
        $$ \Phi = (4 \times 10^{-7}) (4) (\ln 6 - \ln 2) $$
        $$ \Phi = 16 \times 10^{-7} \ln(3) $$
        $$ \Phi \approx 16 \times 10^{-7} (1.0986) \approx 17.58 \times 10^{-7} \text{ Wb} $$
        $$ \Phi = \mathbf{1.758 \mu\text{Wb}} $$

### **7.30 Consider the two-wire transmission line whose cross section is illustrated in Figure 7.36. Each wire is of radius $2 \text{ cm}$, and the wires are separated $10 \text{ cm}$. The wire centered at $(0, 0)$ carries a current of $5 \text{ A}$, while the other centered at $(10 \text{ cm}, 0)$ carries the return current. Find $\mathbf{H}$ at (a) $(5 \text{ cm}, 0)$ and (b) $(10 \text{ cm}, 5 \text{ cm})$.**

*   **Setup:**
    *   **Wire 1:** Center $(0,0)$, Current $I_1 = 5\mathbf{a}_z$ (Assuming standard "out" for positive).
    *   **Wire 2:** Center $(0.1, 0)$ m, Current $I_2 = -5\mathbf{a}_z$ (Return current).
    *   **Note:** We work in meters. $10 \text{ cm} = 0.1 \text{ m}$, $5 \text{ cm} = 0.05 \text{ m}$.

*   **Part (a): $\mathbf{H}$ at $P_1(0.05, 0, 0)$**
    This point is exactly halfway between the wires.
    *   **Contribution from Wire 1:**
        *   Distance $\rho_1 = 0.05$ m.
        *   Direction: Using RHR for current in $+\mathbf{a}_z$, at a point on the $+x$ axis, $\mathbf{H}$ points in $+\mathbf{a}_y$.
        *   $H_1 = \frac{I}{2\pi \rho_1} = \frac{5}{2\pi(0.05)} = \frac{50}{\pi} \text{ A/m}$.

        *   Distance $\rho_2 = 0.05$ m (from $x=0.1$ to $x=0.05$).
        *   Direction: Current is $-\mathbf{a}_z$ (into page). At a point to its *left*, the field circles such that it points up, in $+\mathbf{a}_y$.
        *   $H_2 = \frac{5}{2\pi(0.05)} = \frac{50}{\pi} \text{ A/m}$.
        *   $\mathbf{H}_2 = \frac{50}{\pi} \mathbf{a}_y$.
    *   **Total Field:**
        $$ \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{H}_1 + \mathbf{H}_2 = \frac{100}{\pi} \mathbf{a}_y \approx 31.83 \mathbf{a}_y \text{ A/m} $$

*   **Part (b): $\mathbf{H}$ at $P_2(0.1, 0.05, 0)$**
    This point is directly above Wire 2.
    *   **Contribution from Wire 2:**
        *   Location of wire: $(0.1, 0)$. Point: $(0.1, 0.05)$.
        *   Distance $\rho_2 = 0.05$ m.
        *   Since radius is $2$ cm ($0.02$ m), this point is outside the wire.
        *   Direction: Current $-\mathbf{a}_z$. Radial vector is $+\mathbf{a}_y$. Cross product: $(-\mathbf{a}_z) \times \mathbf{a}_y = \mathbf{a}_x$.
        *   $\mathbf{H}_2 = \frac{I}{2\pi \rho_2} \mathbf{a}_x = \frac{5}{2\pi(0.05)} \mathbf{a}_x = \frac{50}{\pi} \mathbf{a}_x \approx 15.915 \mathbf{a}_x$.

        *   Location of wire: $(0,0)$. Point: $(0.1, 0.05)$.
        *   Vector $\mathbf{r} = 0.1\mathbf{a}_x + 0.05\mathbf{a}_y$.

        *   Current: $5\mathbf{a}_z$.
        *   $\mathbf{H}_1 = \frac{I}{2\pi \rho_1^2} (\mathbf{a}_z \times \mathbf{r}) = \frac{5}{2\pi(0.0125)} (\mathbf{a}_z \times (0.1\mathbf{a}_x + 0.05\mathbf{a}_y))$.
        *   $\mathbf{H}_1 = \frac{5}{0.025\pi} (0.1\mathbf{a}_y - 0.05\mathbf{a}_x) = \frac{200}{\pi} (0.1\mathbf{a}_y - 0.05\mathbf{a}_x)$.
        *   $\mathbf{H}_1 = \frac{20}{\pi}\mathbf{a}_y - \frac{10}{\pi}\mathbf{a}_x$.
        *   $\mathbf{H}_1 \approx 6.366 \mathbf{a}_y - 3.183 \mathbf{a}_x$.
    *   **Total Field:**
        $$ \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{H}_1 + \mathbf{H}_2 = (15.915 - 3.183)\mathbf{a}_x + 6.366\mathbf{a}_y $$
        $$ \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{12.73 \mathbf{a}_x + 6.37 \mathbf{a}_y \text{ A/m}} $$

### **7.42 A current element of length $L$ carries current $I$ in the $z$ direction. Show that at a very distant point, $\mathbf{A} = \frac{\mu_o I L}{4\pi r} \mathbf{a}_z$. Find $\mathbf{B}$.**

*   **Derivation of $\mathbf{A}$:**
    The definition of magnetic vector potential for a line current is:
    $$ \mathbf{A} = \frac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi} \int_L \frac{d\mathbf{l}}{R} $$
    *   For a short element of length $L$ centered at the origin, carrying current in $z$, $d\mathbf{l} = dz \mathbf{a}_z$.
    *   The integral limits are $-L/2$ to $L/2$.
    *   $R$ is the distance from the source point on the wire to the observation point.
    *   **Approximation:** If the point is "very distant" ($r \gg L$), the distance $R$ is approximately equal to the radial distance $r$ from the origin to the point for all segments of the wire.
    *   Thus, $1/R$ can be pulled out of the integral as $1/r$.
    $$ \mathbf{A} \approx \frac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi r} \mathbf{a}_z \int_{-L/2}^{L/2} dz = \frac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi r} [z]_{-L/2}^{L/2} \mathbf{a}_z $$
    $$ \mathbf{A} = \frac{\mu_0 I L}{4\pi r} \mathbf{a}_z $$

*   **Find $\mathbf{B}$:**
    $\mathbf{B} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A}$.
    Since the problem specifies a "distant point" $r$, it is best to use Spherical Coordinates, but $\mathbf{A}$ is given in Cartesian direction $\mathbf{a}_z$. We must convert the vector.
    *   **Vector Transformation:**
        $$ \mathbf{a}_z = \cos\theta \mathbf{a}_r - \sin\theta \mathbf{a}_\theta $$
        $$ \mathbf{A} = \frac{\mu_0 I L}{4\pi r} (\cos\theta \mathbf{a}_r - \sin\theta \mathbf{a}_\theta) $$
        $$ A_r = \frac{\mu_0 I L \cos\theta}{4\pi r}, \quad A_\theta = -\frac{\mu_0 I L \sin\theta}{4\pi r}, \quad A_\phi = 0 $$
    *   **Curl in Spherical Coordinates:**
        $$ \nabla \times \mathbf{A} = \frac{1}{r^2 \sin\theta} \det \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{a}_r & r\mathbf{a}_\theta & r \sin\theta \mathbf{a}_\phi \\ \partial/\partial r & \partial/\partial \theta & \partial/\partial \phi \\ A_r & r A_\theta & r \sin\theta A_\phi \end{vmatrix} $$
        Since $A_\phi = 0$ and $\partial/\partial \phi = 0$ (symmetry):
        $$ \mathbf{B} = \frac{1}{r} \left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial r} (r A_\theta) - \frac{\partial A_r}{\partial \theta} \right] \mathbf{a}_\phi $$
        1.  $r A_\theta = -\frac{\mu_0 I L \sin\theta}{4\pi}$. (Constant w.r.t $r$, so derivative is 0).
        2.  $\frac{\partial A_r}{\partial \theta} = \frac{\mu_0 I L}{4\pi r} (-\sin\theta)$.
        3.  Combine:
        $$ \mathbf{B} = \frac{1}{r} \left[ 0 - \left( -\frac{\mu_0 I L \sin\theta}{4\pi r} \right) \right] \mathbf{a}_\phi $$
        $$ \mathbf{B} = \frac{\mu_0 I L \sin\theta}{4\pi r^2} \mathbf{a}_\phi $$

### **7.43 In free space, $\mathbf{A} = 10 \sin \pi y \mathbf{a}_x + (4 + \cos \pi x)\mathbf{a}_z \text{ Wb/m}$. Find $\mathbf{H}$ and $\mathbf{J}$.**

*   **Find $\mathbf{H}$:**
    We know $\mathbf{B} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A}$ and $\mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{H}$. So $\mathbf{H} = \frac{1}{\mu_0} (\nabla \times \mathbf{A})$.
    $$ \nabla \times \mathbf{A} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{a}_x & \mathbf{a}_y & \mathbf{a}_z \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\ 10 \sin \pi y & 0 & 4 + \cos \pi x \end{vmatrix} $$
    *   $x$-component: $\frac{\partial}{\partial y}(4 + \cos \pi x) - \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(0) = 0$.
    *   $y$-component: $\frac{\partial}{\partial z}(10 \sin \pi y) - \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(4 + \cos \pi x) = 0 - (-\pi \sin \pi x) = \pi \sin \pi x$.
    *   $z$-component: $\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(0) - \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(10 \sin \pi y) = -10\pi \cos \pi y$.
    *   **Result for B:**
        $$ \mathbf{B} = \pi \sin \pi x \mathbf{a}_y - 10\pi \cos \pi y \mathbf{a}_z $$
    *   **Result for H:**
        $$ \mathbf{H} = \frac{\pi}{\mu_0} ( \sin \pi x \mathbf{a}_y - 10 \cos \pi y \mathbf{a}_z ) \text{ A/m} $$

*   **Find $\mathbf{J}$:**
    Using Ampere's Law in point form: $\nabla \times \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{J}$.
    Since $\mu_0$ is constant: $\mathbf{J} = \frac{1}{\mu_0} \nabla \times \mathbf{B}$.
    $$ \nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{a}_x & \mathbf{a}_y & \mathbf{a}_z \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\ 0 & \pi \sin \pi x & -10\pi \cos \pi y \end{vmatrix} $$
    *   $x$-component: $\frac{\partial}{\partial y}(-10\pi \cos \pi y) - \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(\dots) = -10\pi(-\pi \sin \pi y) = 10\pi^2 \sin \pi y$.
    *   $y$-component: $\frac{\partial}{\partial z}(0) - \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(-10\pi \cos \pi y) = 0$.
    *   $z$-component: $\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(\pi \sin \pi x) - \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(0) = \pi(\pi \cos \pi x) = \pi^2 \cos \pi x$.
    *   **Result for J:**
        $$ \mathbf{J} = \frac{\pi^2}{\mu_0} ( 10 \sin \pi y \mathbf{a}_x + \cos \pi x \mathbf{a}_z ) \text{ A/m}^2 $$

### **7.47 The magnetic vector potential of a current distribution in free space is given by $\mathbf{A} = 15e^{-\rho} \sin \phi \mathbf{a}_z \text{ Wb/m}$. Find $\mathbf{H}$ at $(3, \pi/4, -10)$. Calculate the flux through $\rho = 5, 0 \le \phi \le \pi/2, 0 \le z \le 10$.**

*   **Part 1: Find $\mathbf{H}$ at $(3, \pi/4, -10)$**
    First, find $\mathbf{B} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A}$ in cylindrical coordinates.
    $\mathbf{A}$ has only a $z$-component $A_z = 15e^{-\rho} \sin \phi$.
    $$ \mathbf{B} = \frac{1}{\rho}\frac{\partial A_z}{\partial \phi} \mathbf{a}_\rho - \frac{\partial A_z}{\partial \rho} \mathbf{a}_\phi $$
    *   $\frac{\partial A_z}{\partial \phi} = 15e^{-\rho} \cos \phi$.
    *   $\frac{\partial A_z}{\partial \rho} = -15e^{-\rho} \sin \phi$.
    *   Substitute:
        $$ \mathbf{B} = \frac{15e^{-\rho} \cos \phi}{\rho} \mathbf{a}_\rho + 15e^{-\rho} \sin \phi \mathbf{a}_\phi $$

    Now, find $\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{B}/\mu_0$. Evaluate at $\rho = 3, \phi = \pi/4$.
    *   $e^{-\rho} = e^{-3} \approx 0.0498$.
    *   $\cos(\pi/4) = \sin(\pi/4) = 1/\sqrt{2} \approx 0.7071$.
    *   Values:
        *   $B_\rho = \frac{15(0.0498)(0.7071)}{3} = \frac{0.528}{3} = 0.176$.
        *   $B_\phi = 15(0.0498)(0.7071) = 0.528$.
        *   $\mathbf{B} \approx 0.176 \mathbf{a}_\rho + 0.528 \mathbf{a}_\phi$.
    *   $\mathbf{H} = \frac{1}{4\pi \times 10^{-7}} (0.176 \mathbf{a}_\rho + 0.528 \mathbf{a}_\phi)$.
    *   $\mathbf{H} = \frac{10^7}{4\pi} (0.176 \mathbf{a}_\rho + 0.528 \mathbf{a}_\phi)$.
    *   $\frac{10^7}{12.566} \approx 795774$.
    *   $H_\rho \approx 1.4 \times 10^5 \text{ A/m}$.
    *   $H_\phi \approx 4.2 \times 10^5 \text{ A/m}$.
    *   **Exact expression:**
        $$ \mathbf{H} = \frac{15 e^{-3}}{\mu_0 \sqrt{2}} \left( \frac{1}{3}\mathbf{a}_\rho + \mathbf{a}_\phi \right) $$

*   **Part 2: Flux $\Phi$ through surface $\rho = 5, 0 \le \phi \le \pi/2, 0 \le z \le 10$**
    The surface is a curved sheet at constant radius $\rho$.
    *   **Surface Normal:** $d\mathbf{S} = \rho \, d\phi \, dz \, \mathbf{a}_\rho = 5 \, d\phi \, dz \, \mathbf{a}_\rho$.
    *   **Field Component:** We only need the $\mathbf{a}_\rho$ component of $\mathbf{B}$ found earlier.
        $$ B_\rho = \frac{15e^{-\rho} \cos \phi}{\rho} $$
        At $\rho = 5$:
        $$ B_\rho = \frac{15e^{-5} \cos \phi}{5} = 3e^{-5} \cos \phi $$
    *   **Integral:**
        $$ \Phi = \int_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \int_{z=0}^{10} \int_{\phi=0}^{\pi/2} (3e^{-5} \cos \phi) (5 \, d\phi \, dz) $$
        $$ \Phi = 15e^{-5} \int_{0}^{10} dz \int_{0}^{\pi/2} \cos \phi \, d\phi $$
        $$ \Phi = 15e^{-5} [z]_0^{10} [\sin \phi]_0^{\pi/2} $$
        $$ \Phi = 15e^{-5} (10) (1 - 0) = 150e^{-5} $$
    *   **Numerical Value:**
        $$ e^{-5} \approx 0.006738 $$
        $$ \Phi = 150(0.006738) \approx 1.01 \text{ Wb} $$
